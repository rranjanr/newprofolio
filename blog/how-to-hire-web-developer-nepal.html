How to Hire a Reliable Web Developer in Nepal (2026)

Rahul Ranjan · June 13, 2026 · 9 min read · Back to Blog

Nepal's web development scene is growing - which means more options, more confusion, and more chances to get burned by an inexperienced developer who takes the money and delivers a broken site. This guide tells you exactly how to find, vet, and work with a developer who will actually deliver.

Where to Find Web Developers in Nepal

The best developers don't advertise on Facebook groups. Here's where to actually look:

  • Google search: "web developer Nepal portfolio" or "full stack developer Kathmandu/Biratnagar." A developer who invests in their own SEO and portfolio is a professional - that's a signal.
  • LinkedIn: Search "web developer Nepal" and filter by connections. Second-degree connections with a mutual are higher trust.
  • GitHub: Check if they have public repos with recent commits. Dead or empty profiles are a warning sign.
  • Referrals: Ask other Nepal business owners who built their site. Word-of-mouth is the most reliable filter.
  • Upwork/Fiverr: Valid for small tasks, but for a full project you want someone with direct accountability - not a platform profile.

10 Questions to Ask Before You Hire

These questions separate professionals from beginners. A good developer will have confident, specific answers to all of them:

  1. "Can I see 3 live websites you've built?" Not mockups. Not screenshots. Live URLs.
  2. "What tech stack will you use, and why?" They should be able to explain the choice in plain language.
  3. "Who owns the code and files when we're done?" You do. Always. If they hesitate, walk away.
  4. "Will the site be mobile-responsive?" In 2026, this is non-negotiable. If they say "yes, extra charge," leave.
  5. "Who handles hosting and what are the ongoing costs?" You need to know the total cost, not just the build cost.
  6. "How many revisions are included?" Get this in writing before you start.
  7. "What's your process for gathering requirements?" Good developers ask lots of questions before quoting. If they quote immediately, be skeptical.
  8. "How will you handle bugs after launch?" There will be bugs. Know the plan before you need it.
  9. "What's the payment structure?" Standard is 50% upfront, 50% on delivery. Never pay 100% upfront.
  10. "What's your communication style and availability?" Weekly updates? Daily? Slack? Set expectations early.

Red Flags to Watch Out For

Some warning signs are subtle. Others are obvious. Here's what to avoid:

No live portfolio or GitHub activity. Screenshots are easily faked. Ask for live URLs you can inspect.
Quotes a price without asking any questions. A developer who quotes NPR 30,000 in 5 minutes without understanding your project doesn't know what they're building.
Promises #1 Google ranking in 30 days. No one can guarantee this. Anyone who does is lying.
Asks for 100% payment upfront. 50% upfront, 50% on delivery is standard. Never pay in full before you have the keys to your site.
Vague on who owns the code. You own everything. Code, design, domain, hosting - all of it. If they hesitate on this, the relationship will be painful.
Disappears for days without updates. This is the most common complaint about Nepal developers. Set update expectations before you start. A weekly update message takes 5 minutes.

How to Review a Developer's Portfolio

Don't just look at whether the site looks nice. Here's what to actually check:

  • Mobile responsiveness: Open each portfolio site on your phone. Does it work? Or does it look broken?
  • Page load speed: Does it feel fast? A developer who can't optimize their own client's site won't optimize yours.
  • Contact form functionality: Actually test it. Is there spam protection?
  • Code quality (if you can check it): Right-click → "View Source" on any page. Is it organized and readable?
  • Recency: When was the last project? A portfolio with nothing newer than 2022 may indicate they're inactive.
  • Variety: Have they built different types of sites (business, e-commerce, web app)? Or only one type?

Freelancer vs Agency: Pros and Cons

Freelancer

Pros

  • ✓ 30–50% cheaper for same output
  • ✓ Direct access to the person building it
  • ✓ Faster feedback and decisions
  • ✓ More flexible on scope changes

Cons

Pros

  • ✓ Better for very large projects
  • ✓ More formal contracts and processes

Cons

  • ✗ 2–3× more expensive
  • ✗ Less direct communication
  • ✗ Junior devs often do the actual work

Setting Realistic Timeline Expectations

The most common disappointment in Nepal web development projects is timeline mismatch. Here's what real timelines look like:

Project TypeRealistic TimelineWhat Slows It Down
Basic business website1–2 weeksLate content delivery from client
E-commerce site3–6 weeksPayment gateway approvals, product data
Custom web app2–4 monthsScope creep, API integrations
SaaS platform3–6 monthsArchitecture decisions, multi-tenancy

The biggest delay in 90% of projects: the client not delivering content (copy, images, logos). Have your content ready before development starts, or expect to add 2–4 weeks to any timeline.

Working with a Developer: Communication Best Practices

  • Agree on a communication channel upfront. Email, WhatsApp, or Viber - pick one and use it consistently.
  • Request weekly updates in writing. Even a brief "here's what I did this week" message creates accountability.
  • Give feedback within 48 hours. When a developer shares a draft or question, slow responses from your side cause delays.
  • Write down scope changes. "While you're at it, can you add..." costs money and time. Every addition should be discussed and priced separately.
  • Don't judge by looks alone in the early stages. A half-built site looks ugly. Wait until near completion before evaluating aesthetics.
